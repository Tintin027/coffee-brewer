# Coffee Bean Origins and Processing

## The Coffee Belt

Coffee grows in tropical regions between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, known as the Coffee Belt. The two main commercial species are Arabica (higher quality, more complex flavors, grown at higher altitudes) and Robusta (higher caffeine, more bitter, used in blends and instant coffee). Specialty coffee is almost exclusively Arabica.

## Major Origins

### Ethiopia
Ethiopia is the birthplace of coffee and produces some of the most distinctive and prized beans in the world. Ethiopian coffees are known for their fruit-forward, floral, and complex flavor profiles. Major growing regions include Yirgacheffe (known for jasmine, bergamot, and citrus notes), Sidamo/Sidama (berry, wine-like, chocolate), and Guji (tropical fruit, bright acidity). Natural processed Ethiopian coffees are famous for their intense blueberry and strawberry flavors. Ethiopian beans are excellent for pour over methods that highlight their complexity.

### Colombia
Colombia produces consistently high-quality, well-balanced coffee. Colombian beans are known for their medium body, bright acidity, and flavors of caramel, nuts, red fruit, and chocolate. Major regions include Huila (fruity, complex), Nariño (bright, citrusy), and Antioquia (nutty, chocolatey). Colombian coffee works well across virtually all brew methods and is a safe starting point for anyone new to specialty coffee.

### Brazil
Brazil is the world's largest coffee producer. Brazilian coffees tend to be low-acid, full-bodied, and sweet, with flavors of chocolate, nuts, caramel, and dried fruit. They form the base of many espresso blends because their sweetness and body complement other origins. Single-origin Brazilian beans shine in espresso, French press, and cold brew where their chocolate and nut flavors are showcased.

### Kenya
Kenyan coffee is known for its intense, juicy acidity and complex fruit flavors. Expect blackcurrant, tomato, grapefruit, and tropical fruit notes. Kenyan beans are graded by size (AA being the largest and most sought-after). The SL28 and SL34 cultivars are famous for their distinctive flavors. Kenyan coffees are exceptional as pour overs and benefit from lighter roasts that preserve their vibrant acidity.

### Guatemala
Guatemalan coffee is diverse due to the country's varied microclimates. Common flavor profiles include chocolate, caramel, apple, citrus, and floral notes. Antigua is the most famous region, producing full-bodied coffees with cocoa and spice notes. Huehuetenango produces brighter, more fruity coffees at higher altitudes. Guatemalan beans are versatile and work well for espresso and pour over.

### Costa Rica
Costa Rican coffee is known for its bright acidity, clean cup, and honey sweetness. Expect flavors of citrus, honey, stone fruit, and brown sugar. The Tarrazú region is the most well-known. Honey-processed Costa Rican coffees have become increasingly popular for their enhanced sweetness and fruity character.

### Indonesia (Sumatra, Java, Sulawesi)
Indonesian coffees are distinctive for their earthy, herbal, and full-bodied profiles. Sumatran coffee in particular is known for its low acidity, heavy body, and flavors of dark chocolate, tobacco, cedar, and earth. The wet-hulling process (Giling Basah) used in Sumatra contributes to this unique character. Indonesian coffees are excellent in French press and espresso, especially in darker roasts.

## Processing Methods

### Washed (Wet) Process
In washed processing, the fruit is removed from the coffee cherry before drying. The beans are depulped, fermented in water to remove the mucilage, washed clean, and then dried. This produces the cleanest, brightest cup with the most clarity in flavor. Washed coffees tend to highlight origin character and terroir. Most specialty coffee is washed.

### Natural (Dry) Process
In natural processing, the whole coffee cherry is dried with the fruit still attached to the bean. As the fruit dries and ferments, it imparts fruity, wine-like, and fermented flavors to the bean. Natural processed coffees are known for intense berry, tropical fruit, and jammy flavors with heavier body. Ethiopian naturals are the most famous examples. Quality can be inconsistent if not carefully controlled.

### Honey Process
Honey processing is a hybrid approach. The skin of the cherry is removed, but some or all of the mucilage (the sticky, sweet layer) is left on the bean during drying. The amount of mucilage left determines the type — white honey (least mucilage, cleanest), yellow honey, red honey, and black honey (most mucilage, most fruit influence). Honey processed coffees balance the clarity of washed with the sweetness and body of naturals.

### Anaerobic and Experimental Processes
Anaerobic processing involves fermenting coffee in sealed, oxygen-free tanks. This controlled fermentation can produce intense and unusual flavors — boozy, tropical, candy-like. It has become a major trend in specialty coffee and competitive events. Other experimental methods include carbonic maceration (borrowed from winemaking), lactic fermentation, and various fruit co-fermentations.

## Roast Levels

### Light Roast
Light roast beans are roasted to first crack or just past it. They retain more of the origin character, acidity, and complexity. The bean surface is dry (no oil). Light roasts are preferred for specialty single-origin coffees, especially for pour over brewing. They require more precise brewing to taste good and can taste sour if under-extracted.

### Medium Roast
Medium roasts are roasted between first crack and second crack. They balance origin character with roast-developed sweetness and body. You get caramel, chocolate, and nutty notes alongside the origin flavors. Medium roasts are the most versatile and work well across all brew methods.

### Dark Roast
Dark roasts are roasted at or past second crack. The surface is oily and the dominant flavors come from the roasting process rather than the origin — smoky, bittersweet chocolate, caramel, and roasted nuts. Dark roasts are traditional for espresso in Italian-style preparations and for French press. They are more forgiving of imprecise brewing.

## Freshness and Storage

Coffee is at its best between 7-30 days after roasting. Very freshly roasted coffee (under 5 days) has too much CO2 and can taste sharp and undeveloped — this is called degassing. After about 4-6 weeks, beans begin to go stale and lose their aromatic complexity.

Store beans in an airtight container at room temperature, away from light, heat, and moisture. Do not store in the fridge — the moisture and odors will damage the beans. For long-term storage (more than a month), sealed bags can be frozen. Freeze in single-use portions and do not refreeze after thawing.
