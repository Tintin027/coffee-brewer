# Coffee Brewing Methods

## Pour Over (V60, Kalita Wave, Chemex)

Pour over brewing involves pouring hot water over ground coffee in a filter, allowing gravity to draw the water through the grounds. It produces a clean, bright cup that highlights the nuanced flavors of specialty coffee.

The Hario V60 uses a cone-shaped dripper with a single large hole at the bottom and spiral ridges along the interior walls. These ridges create air channels that allow the coffee to drain freely. The V60 requires a medium-fine grind, similar to table salt. A standard recipe is 15g of coffee to 250ml of water (1:16.7 ratio). Water temperature should be between 90-96°C (195-205°F). Total brew time should be around 2:30 to 3:30 minutes.

A typical V60 technique involves blooming the grounds with twice the weight of coffee in water (e.g., 30g water for 15g coffee) for 30-45 seconds. This allows CO2 to escape from freshly roasted coffee. After the bloom, pour in slow concentric circles from the center outward, avoiding pouring directly on the filter walls.

The Kalita Wave uses a flat-bottom dripper with three small drainage holes. It is more forgiving than the V60 because the flat bed promotes even extraction. Use a medium grind, slightly coarser than V60. The same 1:16-1:17 ratio works well. Brew time is typically 3:00-4:00 minutes.

The Chemex uses a thick bonded paper filter that removes more oils and fine particles than other pour over methods. This produces an exceptionally clean cup. Use a medium-coarse grind. The ratio is typically 1:15 to 1:17. Brew time runs 3:30-4:30 minutes. The thick filter means you may need to grind slightly coarser to maintain good flow rate.

## French Press (Immersion Brewing)

French press is a full immersion method where coffee grounds steep in hot water for a set time before being separated by pressing a metal mesh plunger. It produces a heavy, full-bodied cup with more oils and sediment than filtered methods.

Use a coarse grind, similar to sea salt or raw sugar. The standard ratio is 1:15 (e.g., 30g coffee to 450ml water). Water temperature should be 93-96°C (200-205°F). Steep for 4 minutes, then press slowly and pour immediately to avoid over-extraction.

James Hoffmann's technique involves steeping for 4 minutes, then breaking the crust that forms on top with a spoon, scooping off the foam and floating grounds, waiting another 5-8 minutes for the fine particles to settle, then pressing the plunger just below the surface without pressing to the bottom. This produces a much cleaner cup than the traditional method.

## AeroPress

The AeroPress is a versatile manual brewer that uses pressure to push water through a compact puck of coffee. It can produce anything from espresso-style concentrates to clean filter-like cups depending on technique.

The standard (upright) method uses a fine-medium grind, 14-18g of coffee, and water at 80-90°C. Place the filter in the cap, attach to the AeroPress, place on a mug, add coffee, add water, stir for 10 seconds, and press for 20-30 seconds. Total brew time is about 1:30-2:00 minutes.

The inverted method flips the AeroPress upside down so that the coffee steeps without draining. Use 15-18g of coffee with a medium grind. Add water at 85-92°C, stir, steep for 1:00-2:00 minutes, then carefully flip onto a mug and press. This method gives more control over steep time and often produces a fuller-bodied cup.

## Espresso

Espresso is a concentrated coffee brewed by forcing hot water through finely ground coffee at high pressure (typically 9 bars). It forms the base for many popular drinks like lattes, cappuccinos, and americanos.

A standard espresso uses 18-20g of finely ground coffee (dose) to produce 36-40g of liquid espresso (yield) in 25-30 seconds (brew time). This is roughly a 1:2 ratio. Water temperature is typically 90-96°C (195-205°F). The grind should be very fine, almost powdery but not quite — it should clump slightly when pinched.

Dialing in espresso means adjusting grind size, dose, and yield to achieve the desired taste. If the shot runs too fast (under 20 seconds), the grind is too coarse. If it runs too slow (over 35 seconds), the grind is too fine. A properly extracted shot should have a layer of golden-brown crema on top.

## Moka Pot

The Moka pot (stovetop espresso maker) brews coffee by passing boiling water pressurized by steam through ground coffee. It produces a strong, concentrated brew that is not true espresso but stronger than drip coffee.

Use a medium-fine grind, finer than drip but coarser than espresso. Fill the bottom chamber with hot water up to the safety valve. Fill the filter basket with coffee but do not tamp — just level it off. Use medium-low heat. The coffee should flow out slowly and steadily. Remove from heat when you hear a hissing/gurgling sound, which means the water is almost gone. Brewing takes about 4-5 minutes on the stove.

## Cold Brew

Cold brew is made by steeping coarsely ground coffee in cold or room temperature water for an extended period, typically 12-24 hours. It produces a smooth, low-acid concentrate that can be diluted with water or milk.

Use a coarse grind and a ratio of 1:8 for concentrate (which you dilute before drinking) or 1:15 for ready-to-drink strength. Steep in the fridge for 12-24 hours. Strain through a fine mesh filter or cheesecloth, then through a paper filter for extra clarity. The concentrate keeps in the fridge for up to 2 weeks.
