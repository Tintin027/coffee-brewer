# Coffee Recipes

## Classic V60 Pour Over (James Hoffmann Method)

Equipment: Hario V60, V60 paper filter, gooseneck kettle, scale, timer
Coffee: 15g, medium-fine grind
Water: 250g at 95°C
Ratio: 1:16.7

Steps:
1. Rinse the paper filter with hot water to remove paper taste and preheat the dripper. Discard rinse water.
2. Add 15g of ground coffee. Create a small well in the center.
3. Start timer. Pour 50g of water (bloom), swirl gently to saturate all grounds.
4. Wait until 0:45.
5. At 0:45, begin pouring slowly in concentric circles. Pour to 150g by 1:15.
6. At 1:15, continue pouring to 250g by 1:45.
7. Give the dripper a gentle swirl after the last pour to flatten the coffee bed.
8. Let it drain completely. Target total brew time: 2:30-3:30.
9. Swirl the finished coffee in the server to mix, then serve.

Notes: If brew time is under 2:30, grind finer. Over 3:30, grind coarser. The bed should be flat and even after draining — an uneven bed indicates inconsistent pouring.

## AeroPress (Inverted, Balanced Recipe)

Equipment: AeroPress, paper or metal filter, kettle, scale, timer, stirrer
Coffee: 16g, medium grind
Water: 240g at 88°C
Ratio: 1:15

Steps:
1. Insert the plunger into the AeroPress about 1cm and flip upside down (inverted position).
2. Add 16g of ground coffee.
3. Start timer. Pour 240g of water.
4. Stir 3-4 times gently.
5. Place the filter cap with a rinsed paper filter on top. Let steep until 2:00.
6. At 2:00, carefully flip the AeroPress onto your mug.
7. Press slowly and steadily for 20-30 seconds. Stop pressing when you hear the hiss.

Notes: The lower water temperature (88°C) helps avoid over-extraction with the medium grind and longer steep time. For a stronger cup, increase the dose to 18g or use a finer grind.

## French Press (Hoffmann Method)

Equipment: French press, kettle, scale, timer, spoon
Coffee: 30g, coarse grind
Water: 500g at 96°C
Ratio: 1:16.7

Steps:
1. Add 30g of coarsely ground coffee to the French press.
2. Start timer. Pour all 500g of water.
3. Let steep for 4:00 without touching it. A crust of grounds will form on the surface.
4. At 4:00, use a spoon to break the crust by stirring gently. Most grounds will sink.
5. Scoop off any foam and floating particles from the surface with two spoons.
6. Wait another 5-8 minutes. This allows fine particles to settle to the bottom.
7. Place the plunger on top but do not press down — just use it as a strainer.
8. Pour gently, leaving the last bit in the press to avoid sediment.

Notes: This produces a much cleaner French press cup than the traditional 4-minute steep and full press. Total time is 9-12 minutes, but the result is worth the wait. Do not plunge to the bottom.

## Espresso (Standard Recipe)

Equipment: Espresso machine, grinder, tamper, scale, timer
Coffee: 18g, fine grind
Yield: 36g liquid espresso
Time: 25-30 seconds
Ratio: 1:2

Steps:
1. Grind 18g of coffee directly into the portafilter basket.
2. Distribute grounds evenly. Use WDT (stir with a thin needle) for best results.
3. Tamp firmly and level — about 15kg of pressure. The surface should be smooth and flat.
4. Lock the portafilter into the group head.
5. Start the shot and timer simultaneously.
6. The shot should begin with slow, dark drips, then transition to a steady honey-like stream.
7. Stop the shot at 36g of liquid espresso. Target time: 25-30 seconds.

Dialing in: If the shot runs under 25 seconds, grind finer. Over 30 seconds, grind coarser. Taste is the ultimate judge — a well-extracted espresso should be sweet, balanced, and have a pleasant aftertaste.

## Cold Brew Concentrate

Equipment: Large jar or cold brew pitcher, fine mesh strainer, paper filter, scale
Coffee: 100g, coarse grind
Water: 800g, cold or room temperature
Ratio: 1:8 (for concentrate)

Steps:
1. Add 100g of coarsely ground coffee to a large jar or pitcher.
2. Pour 800g of cold or room temperature water over the grounds.
3. Stir gently to make sure all grounds are saturated.
4. Cover and place in the refrigerator.
5. Steep for 16-20 hours.
6. Strain through a fine mesh strainer to remove large grounds.
7. Strain again through a paper filter for a cleaner concentrate (optional but recommended).
8. Store concentrate in the fridge. It keeps for up to 2 weeks.

Serving: Dilute the concentrate 1:1 with water or milk. Pour over ice. Adjust dilution to taste — some people prefer 1:2 (concentrate to water) for a lighter cup. The concentrate also works great as a base for iced lattes.

## Iced Pour Over (Japanese Method)

Equipment: V60 (or any pour over), paper filter, gooseneck kettle, scale, timer
Coffee: 20g, medium-fine grind (slightly finer than usual hot V60)
Water: 150g hot water at 95°C + 120g ice
Total liquid: ~250g
Ratio: 1:13.5 (total water including ice)

Steps:
1. Place 120g of ice in the server or carafe below the dripper.
2. Rinse the paper filter with hot water. Discard rinse water (keep the ice).
3. Add 20g of ground coffee to the dripper.
4. Start timer. Bloom with 40g of hot water for 45 seconds.
5. Pour remaining 110g of hot water in slow circles, finishing by 2:00.
6. Let drain. The hot coffee drips directly onto the ice, cooling instantly.
7. Swirl to melt remaining ice and mix. Serve immediately over fresh ice if desired.

Notes: Brewing directly onto ice locks in aromatic compounds that would otherwise evaporate in hot coffee left to cool. The result is brighter and more complex than cold brew. Grind slightly finer than normal to compensate for the reduced amount of hot water.

## Moka Pot Stovetop Brew

Equipment: Moka pot, kettle, stove, scale
Coffee: Fill the filter basket completely (varies by moka pot size, typically 15-20g for a 3-cup), medium-fine grind
Water: Fill to below the safety valve with hot water

Steps:
1. Boil water separately in a kettle.
2. Fill the bottom chamber of the moka pot with hot water up to just below the safety valve.
3. Fill the filter basket with coffee. Level it off with your finger — do not tamp.
4. Insert the filter basket and screw on the top chamber. Use a towel to hold the hot bottom.
5. Place on the stove at medium-low heat. Leave the lid open so you can watch.
6. Coffee will begin to flow from the spout after 2-3 minutes. It should flow steadily, not sputter.
7. When the flow turns pale and starts sputtering, remove from heat immediately.
8. Run the bottom of the moka pot under cold water to stop extraction.
9. Pour and enjoy. Can be served as-is or diluted with hot water for an americano-style drink.

Notes: Starting with hot water reduces the time the coffee spends on heat, preventing bitter, burnt flavors. Never use high heat. The coffee should emerge dark and honey-like, not pale and watery.
