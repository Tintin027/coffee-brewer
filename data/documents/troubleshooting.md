# Coffee Troubleshooting Guide

## Sour Coffee

Sour or acidic-tasting coffee is a sign of under-extraction. The water hasn't dissolved enough of the soluble compounds from the grounds, leaving behind the bright, acidic flavors that extract first while missing the sweetness and body that come later.

Possible fixes for sour coffee: Grind finer to increase extraction by exposing more surface area. Increase brew time by pouring slower or steeping longer. Increase water temperature — cooler water extracts less. Check your coffee-to-water ratio — using too much coffee relative to water can lead to under-extraction. Make sure your coffee isn't too fresh — very freshly roasted beans (under 5 days) can produce excessive sourness due to CO2.

## Bitter Coffee

Bitter coffee is typically a sign of over-extraction. The water has dissolved too many compounds, pulling out the harsh, bitter, and astringent flavors that extract last.

Possible fixes for bitter coffee: Grind coarser to reduce extraction. Shorten brew time. Lower water temperature. Use less coffee or more water. Check that you're not letting coffee sit on heat after brewing (this causes continued extraction and bitterness). Make sure your equipment is clean — old coffee oils that accumulate on grinders and brewers become rancid and add bitterness.

## Watery or Weak Coffee

If your coffee tastes thin, weak, or watery, you're either not extracting enough or not using enough coffee.

Possible fixes: Increase the dose (use more coffee). Grind finer to increase extraction. Make sure your water is hot enough — ideally 90-96°C (195-205°F). If using a pour over, make sure you're not channeling (water finding a path around the grounds instead of through them). Stir or agitate to ensure all grounds are saturated.

## Astringent or Dry Mouthfeel

Astringency is a dry, puckering sensation similar to over-steeped tea. It is a specific type of over-extraction that goes beyond simple bitterness.

Possible fixes: Grind significantly coarser. Reduce water temperature to around 88-92°C. Reduce agitation during brewing. For pour over, avoid pouring directly on the filter walls — this causes water to bypass the coffee bed. Make sure your coffee isn't roasted too dark for your brew method.

## Muddy or Silty Coffee

A muddy cup with visible sediment is usually caused by too-fine grind, broken filters, or improper filtering technique.

Possible fixes: Grind coarser. For French press, try the Hoffmann method — wait 5-8 minutes after pressing for fines to settle, and don't press all the way to the bottom. Make sure your paper filters are properly seated and not folded or torn. If using a metal filter, accept that some sediment is normal — switch to paper if you want full clarity.

## Channeling (Espresso)

Channeling occurs when water finds a path of least resistance through the coffee puck instead of flowing evenly through the entire bed. This causes some areas to over-extract while others under-extract, resulting in a shot that tastes both sour and bitter.

Signs of channeling: uneven flow from the portafilter, spraying or squirting, blonde spots in the puck after extraction, wildly inconsistent shot times.

Possible fixes: Distribute grounds evenly before tamping using WDT (Weiss Distribution Technique) — stir the grounds in the basket with a thin needle or WDT tool. Tamp level and consistent. Make sure your grind is even — an upgrade to a better grinder often fixes channeling. Check that your portafilter basket is clean and not clogged.

## Slow Drawdown (Pour Over)

If your pour over is taking much longer than expected, the grind is too fine, creating a clogged bed that water can't pass through efficiently.

Possible fixes: Grind coarser. Pour with less agitation — aggressive pouring stirs up fines that clog the filter. Try a single pour technique instead of multiple pours. If using a V60, a faster pour can help — the weight of water above helps push through. Make sure you're using the correct filter for your brewer.

## Fast Drawdown (Pour Over)

If water rushes through your pour over too quickly, extraction will be insufficient.

Possible fixes: Grind finer. Pour more slowly to extend contact time. Use a brewer with more flow restriction (Kalita Wave is more forgiving than V60). Make sure you're using the right amount of coffee — too little coffee creates a thin bed that water passes through easily.

## Coffee Tastes Flat or Stale

If your coffee lacks aroma and tastes papery, cardboard-like, or simply dull, the beans are likely past their prime.

Possible fixes: Buy freshly roasted coffee — look for a roast date on the bag and use within 2-6 weeks of roasting. Store beans in an airtight container away from light, heat, and moisture. Do not store beans in the fridge or freezer for daily use (freezer is okay for long-term storage of sealed bags). Grind immediately before brewing.

## Inconsistent Results

If every cup tastes different even with the same recipe, the issue is usually inconsistency in one or more variables.

Possible fixes: Use a scale to measure coffee and water precisely every time. Use a thermometer or temperature-controlled kettle. Use a timer. Invest in a burr grinder for consistent particle size. Develop a consistent pouring technique — practice the same pattern each time. Keep variables constant and only change one thing at a time when troubleshooting.
