# Coffee Grind Size Guide

## Why Grind Size Matters

Grind size is one of the most important variables in coffee brewing. It directly controls the rate of extraction — how quickly water dissolves the soluble compounds in coffee. Finer grinds have more surface area exposed to water, so they extract faster. Coarser grinds extract slower.

If your grind is too fine for your brew method, you'll over-extract, producing bitter, harsh, astringent flavors. If your grind is too coarse, you'll under-extract, resulting in sour, thin, underdeveloped flavors. The goal is to find the grind size that produces balanced extraction for your specific method and brew time.

## Grind Size Reference Chart

Extra Coarse — like peppercorns or coarse sea salt. Used for cold brew and cowboy coffee. Very chunky and visibly distinct particles.

Coarse — like raw sugar or kosher salt. Used for French press and percolators. You can feel individual particles between your fingers.

Medium-Coarse — like coarse sand. Used for Chemex and clever dripper. Slightly finer than French press but still visibly gritty.

Medium — like regular sand or table salt. Used for flat-bottom drip brewers (Kalita Wave, standard drip machines). The most common "default" grind.

Medium-Fine — like fine sand or slightly finer than table salt. Used for cone-shaped pour overs like V60 and AeroPress (standard method). This is finer than what most pre-ground coffee comes as.

Fine — like powdered sugar or flour, but with slight grit. Used for espresso and Moka pot. Clumps when pinched between fingers.

Extra Fine — like talcum powder. Used for Turkish/Ibrik coffee only. Almost no grit at all.

## Burr Grinders vs Blade Grinders

Burr grinders crush beans between two abrasive surfaces (burrs) set at a specific distance apart. This produces uniform particle size, which leads to even extraction. They are strongly recommended for any serious coffee brewing.

Blade grinders chop beans with a spinning blade, producing highly uneven particle sizes — some dust and some boulders. This leads to simultaneous over-extraction of fines and under-extraction of large pieces, resulting in a muddy, bitter, and sour cup. Avoid blade grinders if possible.

Conical burr grinders are the most common home grinder type. They are quieter and produce less heat but create a slightly wider particle distribution than flat burrs. Popular models include the Baratza Encore, Timemore C2 (hand grinder), and 1Zpresso JX (hand grinder).

Flat burr grinders produce a more uniform particle size and are preferred for espresso. They are louder and generate more heat. Popular home models include the Baratza Vario, Fellow Ode (for filter), and DF64 (for espresso).

## Dialing In Your Grind

Dialing in means adjusting your grind size to optimize extraction for a specific coffee, method, and recipe. Start with the recommended grind size for your brew method, then adjust based on taste.

If your coffee tastes sour, weak, or thin, your grind is likely too coarse. The water is passing through too quickly without extracting enough. Grind finer.

If your coffee tastes bitter, harsh, dry, or astringent, your grind is likely too fine. The water is extracting too many undesirable compounds. Grind coarser.

Make one adjustment at a time. Change the grind by one or two notches on your grinder, keep everything else the same, and taste the result. Repeat until you hit the sweet spot.

For pour over methods, brew time is a useful proxy. If your V60 drains in under 2 minutes, grind finer. If it takes over 4 minutes, grind coarser. Target 2:30-3:30 for most V60 recipes.

For espresso, shot time is critical. If your shot pulls in under 20 seconds, grind finer. Over 35 seconds, grind coarser. Target 25-30 seconds for a standard 1:2 ratio shot.

## Grind Freshness

Coffee begins losing aroma and flavor within minutes of being ground because grinding exposes the interior of the bean to oxygen. Whole beans stay fresh much longer than ground coffee.

For the best results, grind your coffee immediately before brewing. If you must grind ahead, store ground coffee in an airtight container and use within a few days. Pre-ground coffee from the store has already lost significant flavor, though it can still make a decent cup for less demanding methods like French press or cold brew.
